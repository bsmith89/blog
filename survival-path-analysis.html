Beyond correlation: Bayesian survival path analysis

Exploring SCFAs as mediators of increased survival in acarbose-treated mice

published: Saturday, May 12, 2018
category: Data
tags:
  • acarbose
  • SCFAs
  • causal inference
  • survival analysis

In the first chapter of my thesis ([preprint here][preprint]) I explored the hypothesis that some of the longevity extending effect of acarbose in mice is mediated by the gut microbiota and the short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) products of polysaccharide fermentation. Our key finding was a multivariate association between mouse longevity and the primary SCFAs, propionate, butyrate, and acetate. This is consistent with the well established health effects of these metabolites.

To statistically model this association, and ultimately to carry out null-hypothesis testing, we used proportional hazards regression, a broadly used tool in the domain of survival analysis. This approach enables linear-regression-like analysis of time-to-event data (including but not limited to time-to-death), which may also be censored, truncated, or be affected by multiple covariates.

Conceptually, the proportional hazards model assumes a baseline hazard function, $\lambda(t)$, describing the hazard (e.g. risk of death) during a particular period of time, conditional on survival up to that period. This function can be directly transformed into a probability distribution on survival.

In the notation of statistical modeling:

$ y \sim \mathrm{Survival}(\lambda) $

$ \lambda_0(t) = \mathrm{P}(d_t | \neg d_{t < 1}) $

When covariates are added, the model assumes that they have proportional effects on hazard and that these effects are equal during every period of time.

$ \lambda(t) = \lambda_0(t) * e^{X \beta} $

Inference in this model is based on the values of the beta parameters; P-values reflect the statistical significance of their difference from 0 (no effect).

Using that model as implemented in R's "survival" package, our null model included terms for sex, treatment, and which of the study sites the mouse was housed at, what I call the "design" covariates, since they were experimentally controlled. We then compare that to an alternative model in which we add parameters for the concentration of propionate, butyrate, and acetate in feces. We found, using a likelihood ratio test, that the alternative model fit the data significantly better than the null, suggesting that predictions of mouse longevity are improved when SCFA concentrations are known.

While this kind of statistical association is one prediction of our hypothesis, it does not provide, on its own, substantial evidence for a causal link going from these fermentation products to lifespan. We did not experimentally control SCFA concentrations directly, instead relying on their increase as a "side-effect" of acarbose treatment. Since acarbose has other major impacts on host physiology, we cannot rule these out as the true cause of increased lifespan. This is primarily because the same association could be explained by acarbose increasing both SCFA concentrations and lifespan.

Interestingly, after sharing our preprint on Twitter, Sheldon Rowan pointed me to the supplemental results of his paper (see Figure S1 Panel C), where mice fed a more slowly digested, high-amylose diet—which may also present more polysaccharide for bacterial fermentation in the lower gut—live longer than mice fed a more quickly digested polysaccharide diet.

  • We found an association between SCFAs and longevity (point to the preprint)
    • This is consistent with a causal role for SCFAs, but also with the obvious "common cause" hypothesis: ACA
  • Including SCFAs in regression replaces ACA as a significant predictor of survival
    • This is better support for SCFAs as a mediator of ACAs effects on longevity
    • But this is not particularly rigorous, because other things could drive the loss of significance (e.g. colinearity)
